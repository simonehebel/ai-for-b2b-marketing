# Human-in-the-Loop Governance Framework

## Purpose

This framework helps B2B marketing teams design AI-enabled workflows in which human judgment, approval and accountability remain clearly defined.

Human-in-the-loop governance is not only a final review step.

It is an operating model that defines:

- what AI is allowed to do,
- where human judgment is required,
- who reviews and approves outputs,
- which quality standards apply,
- how risks are escalated,
- how decisions are documented,
- who remains accountable for the final business outcome.

## When to Use This Framework

Use this framework when AI supports activities such as:

- customer or market insight synthesis,
- campaign brief development,
- content drafting,
- messaging recommendations,
- segmentation support,
- campaign quality assurance,
- Sales enablement,
- lifecycle and nurture planning,
- reporting and analysis,
- workflow automation.

The framework is especially relevant when AI outputs may influence:

- external communication,
- customer decisions,
- commercial claims,
- targeting,
- campaign investment,
- customer data,
- brand reputation,
- legal or compliance requirements.

## 1. Governance Principles

### Human Accountability

A named person must remain accountable for every AI-enabled workflow and final output.

AI systems do not own business decisions.

### Clear Scope

The role of AI must be clearly limited and documented.

### Proportional Review

The level of human review should reflect the potential business, customer, legal and reputational risk.

### Approved Inputs

Only information approved for the selected platform and workflow should be used.

### Evidence-Based Validation

Claims, facts, statistics and recommendations must be verified against reliable sources.

### Separation of Drafting and Approval

The person generating or operating the workflow should not automatically approve high-risk outputs without additional review.

### Documentation

Important decisions, corrections and approvals should be recorded.

### Continuous Improvement

Feedback from reviewers and users should improve the workflow over time.

## 2. Define the AI Role

Document exactly what AI supports.

### AI-Supported Activities

AI may support:

- summarization,
- classification,
- comparison,
- drafting,
- idea generation,
- pattern identification,
- information structuring,
- quality checks,
- workflow recommendations.

### Activities AI Should Not Own

AI should not independently:

- approve external content,
- publish customer communication,
- make final commercial decisions,
- create unsupported customer claims,
- interpret legal requirements,
- decide which personal data may be used,
- assign accountability,
- replace subject-matter validation,
- determine final targeting without review.

### AI Role Statement

Use this structure:

> AI supports [specific activity] by producing [defined output] from [approved inputs]. The output is reviewed by [role] against [quality criteria]. Final approval and accountability remain with [role].

## 3. Define Workflow Risk Levels

The level of human oversight should depend on the risk of the activity.

### Low Risk

Examples:

- internal brainstorming,
- formatting,
- summarizing non-sensitive internal notes,
- organizing approved information,
- creating first-draft checklists.

Recommended control:

- user review before use.

### Medium Risk

Examples:

- campaign brief drafting,
- customer insight synthesis,
- internal messaging recommendations,
- content quality checks,
- analysis used for campaign planning.

Recommended control:

- review by a trained subject-matter owner,
- documented quality criteria,
- correction before activation.

### High Risk

Examples:

- external customer content,
- product or performance claims,
- personalized communication,
- targeting recommendations,
- use of customer or personal data,
- regulated industry communication,
- executive or investor communication.

Recommended control:

- subject-matter review,
- business-owner approval,
- Legal, Privacy, Security or Compliance review where required,
- documented approval before publication or activation.

### Prohibited or Unacceptable Risk

Examples:

- entering confidential data into unapproved systems,
- automated external publication without review,
- fabricated customer evidence,
- automated legal interpretation,
- discriminatory targeting,
- impersonation,
- decisions without accountable ownership.

Recommended control:

- do not proceed.

## 4. Human Review Roles

### Workflow Owner

Responsible for:

- defining the business purpose,
- maintaining the workflow,
- coordinating stakeholders,
- monitoring value and adoption,
- ensuring governance requirements are followed.

### AI Operator

Responsible for:

- preparing approved inputs,
- running the workflow,
- documenting the prompt or instruction where required,
- identifying obvious output issues,
- forwarding results for review.

### Subject-Matter Reviewer

Responsible for:

- validating technical or business accuracy,
- checking customer relevance,
- identifying unsupported assumptions,
- correcting errors,
- confirming that required context is included.

### Brand or Content Reviewer

Responsible for:

- tone of voice,
- message consistency,
- readability,
- customer relevance,
- content quality.

### Data or Privacy Reviewer

Responsible for:

- personal data handling,
- data classification,
- approved platform use,
- retention requirements,
- privacy compliance.

### Legal or Compliance Reviewer

Responsible for:

- regulated claims,
- contractual or legal risk,
- required disclaimers,
- external communication standards.

### Final Approver

Responsible for:

- accepting or rejecting the output,
- confirming required reviews are complete,
- approving activation or publication,
- remaining accountable for the final use.

## 5. RACI Model

Use the following definitions:

- **Responsible:** completes the activity
- **Accountable:** owns the final result
- **Consulted:** provides required input
- **Informed:** receives relevant updates

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Define business objective | Consulted | Consulted | Accountable | Informed | Informed |
| Approve input sources | Responsible | Consulted | Accountable | Consulted | Informed |
| Run AI workflow | Responsible | Informed | Accountable | Informed | Informed |
| Review factual accuracy | Consulted | Responsible | Accountable | Informed | Informed |
| Review brand and messaging | Consulted | Responsible | Accountable | Informed | Informed |
| Review data and privacy | Informed | Consulted | Responsible | Accountable | Informed |
| Approve external use | Informed | Consulted | Responsible | Consulted | Accountable |
| Document outcome | Responsible | Consulted | Accountable | Informed | Informed |
| Monitor performance | Responsible | Consulted | Accountable | Informed | Informed |

The exact model should reflect the workflow, organization and risk level.

## 6. Input Governance

AI workflows should only use approved and relevant inputs.

### Input Categories

Possible inputs include:

- customer interview notes,
- market research,
- campaign performance data,
- product information,
- approved messaging,
- brand guidelines,
- public sources,
- Sales feedback,
- event feedback,
- existing content.

### Input Review Questions

- Is the information approved for use?
- Is it current?
- Is it accurate?
- Is it complete?
- Does it contain personal information?
- Does it contain confidential information?
- Is the AI platform approved for this information?
- Who owns the data?
- How long may the information be retained?

## 7. Output Quality Criteria

Every workflow should define what a usable output looks like.

### Accuracy

- facts are correct,
- conclusions are supported,
- no information is invented,
- sources are traceable where required.

### Relevance

- output addresses the business objective,
- customer context is reflected,
- unnecessary information is removed,
- recommendations are actionable.

### Completeness

- required sections are present,
- relevant stakeholders are considered,
- known limitations are documented,
- missing information is identified.

### Brand and Messaging

- tone is appropriate,
- terminology is consistent,
- claims are supported,
- communication is suitable for the audience.

### Risk and Compliance

- no restricted information is exposed,
- required approvals are complete,
- legal or regulatory claims are validated,
- privacy requirements are respected.

### Usability

- output can be understood,
- next actions are clear,
- ownership is visible,
- format supports the wider process.

## 8. Review Checklist

Before approving an AI-generated or AI-supported output, confirm:

- [ ] The business objective is clear
- [ ] Only approved inputs were used
- [ ] Sensitive information was handled correctly
- [ ] Factual statements were checked
- [ ] Unsupported claims were removed
- [ ] Customer and market context were validated
- [ ] Required sections are complete
- [ ] Brand and tone requirements are met
- [ ] Bias or inappropriate assumptions were reviewed
- [ ] Legal, Privacy or Compliance review was completed where required
- [ ] The accountable owner approved the output
- [ ] The final version is documented

## 9. Approval Levels

### Level 1: User Review

Appropriate for:

- internal low-risk use,
- brainstorming,
- formatting,
- non-sensitive summaries.

Required approval:

- workflow user.

### Level 2: Subject-Matter Approval

Appropriate for:

- internal campaign planning,
- insight synthesis,
- messaging recommendations,
- content drafts,
- workflow analysis.

Required approval:

- subject-matter owner or marketing owner.

### Level 3: Cross-Functional Approval

Appropriate for:

- external customer content,
- personalized communication,
- product claims,
- customer data use,
- regulated communication.

Required approval may include:

- Marketing owner,
- Product or subject-matter owner,
- Legal,
- Privacy,
- Security,
- Compliance.

### Level 4: Executive Approval

Appropriate for:

- high-impact external statements,
- major commercial commitments,
- executive communication,
- strategically sensitive decisions.

Required approval:

- accountable executive and required specialist functions.

## 10. Escalation Model

Escalate the workflow when:

- output contains unsupported facts,
- sensitive information may have been exposed,
- ownership is unclear,
- reviewers disagree,
- legal or regulatory concerns exist,
- the output may affect customers negatively,
- AI behavior is inconsistent,
- required controls are missing,
- the business risk exceeds the approved scope.

### Escalation Process

1. Stop publication or activation.
2. Document the issue.
3. Identify the affected workflow and output.
4. Notify the workflow owner.
5. Involve required specialist functions.
6. Assess business and customer impact.
7. Correct or withdraw the output.
8. Document the decision.
9. Update the workflow or controls.
10. Confirm whether use may resume.

## 11. Rejection Criteria

An output should be rejected when:

- facts cannot be verified,
- customer evidence is invented,
- sensitive information is exposed,
- required context is missing,
- the output is misleading,
- the tone is inappropriate,
- claims exceed available evidence,
- bias or harmful assumptions are present,
- approval requirements are incomplete,
- the output does not support the business objective.

### Rejection Outcome

When output is rejected, document:

- reason for rejection,
- reviewer,
- required correction,
- workflow issue,
- whether the workflow may be rerun,
- whether escalation is required.

## 13. Feedback and Improvement

Human review should improve the workflow over time.

### Feedback Categories

- missing context,
- incorrect fact,
- unsupported assumption,
- poor structure,
- irrelevant content,
- brand inconsistency,
- legal or compliance concern,
- data quality issue,
- unclear ownership,
- low business value.

### Improvement Questions

- Which errors occur repeatedly?
- Which inputs are missing?
- Which review criteria are unclear?
- Which steps create unnecessary effort?
- Which controls should be strengthened?
- Which tasks should remain fully human?
- Has the workflow created measurable value?
- Are users following the process?

## Human-in-the-Loop Governance Checklist

Before activating an AI-enabled marketing workflow, confirm:

- [ ] The business objective is documented
- [ ] The AI role is clearly limited
- [ ] A workflow owner is assigned
- [ ] Approved inputs are defined
- [ ] Prohibited inputs are documented
- [ ] Risk level is assessed
- [ ] Review roles are assigned
- [ ] Quality criteria are documented
- [ ] Approval level is defined
- [ ] Rejection criteria are clear
- [ ] Escalation process is available
- [ ] Final accountability remains with a person
- [ ] Decisions can be documented
- [ ] Measurement is defined
- [ ] Users understand the workflow

## Key Principle

Human-in-the-loop governance is not an obstacle to AI adoption.

It is the operating model that makes AI use:

- accountable,
- reviewable,
- safe,
- relevant,
- repeatable,
- commercially useful,
- organizationally sustainable.
