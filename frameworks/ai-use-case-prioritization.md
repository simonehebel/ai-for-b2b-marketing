# AI Use Case Prioritization Framework

## Purpose

This framework helps B2B marketing teams identify, assess and prioritize AI use cases based on business value, feasibility, risk and organizational readiness.

The goal is not to find as many AI applications as possible.

The goal is to identify a limited number of use cases that:

- solve a relevant business problem,
- improve an existing marketing process,
- can be implemented with available data and resources,
- maintain appropriate human accountability,
- create measurable value,
- can realistically be adopted by the organization.

## When to Use This Framework

Use this framework when an organization needs to:

- identify practical AI opportunities,
- compare multiple AI use cases,
- avoid tool-led experimentation without business value,
- allocate limited resources,
- establish an AI marketing roadmap,
- distinguish pilots from scalable use cases,
- assess risk before implementation,
- align Marketing, Operations, IT, Legal and leadership.

## 1. Start with the Business Problem

Do not begin with a tool, model or prompt.

First define the business problem or process limitation.

### Business Problem Questions

- Which marketing process is currently inefficient?
- Where is quality inconsistent?
- Where does execution take too long?
- Where is relevant information fragmented?
- Which decisions lack sufficient insight?
- Where does manual work create limited additional value?
- Which process creates repeated rework?
- Which business outcome could improve?

### Business Problem Statement

Use this structure:

> The current process for [activity] creates [problem or limitation], resulting in [business impact]. The objective is to improve [desired outcome] without compromising [quality, compliance or accountability requirement].

### Example

> The current process for synthesizing customer interview findings is manual and inconsistent, resulting in delayed campaign planning and limited reuse of insight. The objective is to improve speed and consistency while maintaining human validation of all conclusions.

## 3. Assess Business Value

Evaluate the expected contribution of the use case.

### Value Dimensions

#### Time Efficiency

- Does the use case reduce repetitive manual effort?
- Does it shorten preparation or execution time?
- Does it improve response speed?

#### Quality

- Does it improve consistency?
- Does it reduce avoidable errors?
- Does it support more complete analysis?
- Does it improve relevance?

#### Scalability

- Can the workflow support more markets, segments or campaigns?
- Can outputs be reused?
- Can the process be standardized?

#### Commercial Relevance

- Does it support pipeline, conversion or customer engagement?
- Does it improve Sales and Marketing alignment?
- Does it strengthen customer understanding?
- Does it improve decision quality?

#### Organizational Value

- Does it reduce dependency on individual knowledge?
- Does it improve documentation?
- Does it strengthen governance?
- Does it support adoption of better processes?

## 7. Assess Risk

AI use cases should be evaluated for risk before activation.

### Risk Categories

#### Accuracy Risk

- incorrect summaries,
- unsupported conclusions,
- fabricated information,
- outdated facts,
- misinterpretation of customer input.

#### Brand Risk

- inconsistent tone,
- generic messaging,
- inappropriate claims,
- poor-quality external content.

#### Data and Privacy Risk

- confidential information,
- personal data,
- unapproved data processing,
- unclear data retention.

#### Legal and Compliance Risk

- copyright concerns,
- regulatory requirements,
- unsupported product claims,
- missing approvals.

#### Operational Risk

- unclear ownership,
- overreliance on AI,
- inconsistent review,
- lack of documentation,
- poor user adoption.

#### Customer Risk

- insensitive or irrelevant personalization,
- incorrect assumptions,
- inappropriate automated communication,
- loss of trust.

## 11. Priority Categories

### Prioritize

Use cases with:

- strong business value,
- sufficient input quality,
- manageable risk,
- clear human ownership,
- realistic implementation effort,
- measurable outcomes.

Recommended action:

- define pilot,
- confirm owner,
- document success criteria,
- begin controlled implementation.

### Prepare

Use cases with relevant value but important readiness gaps.

Possible gaps include:

- incomplete data,
- unclear review process,
- missing governance,
- insufficient ownership,
- technical dependencies.

Recommended action:

- close critical gaps before implementation.

### Pilot Carefully

Use cases with potential value and moderate uncertainty.

Recommended action:

- limit scope,
- avoid external publication,
- use controlled data,
- require strong human review,
- define clear stop criteria.

### Monitor

Use cases that may become relevant later but currently lack sufficient value or readiness.

Recommended action:

- document assumptions,
- review when inputs, technology or business priorities change.

### Do Not Proceed

Use cases with:

- unclear business value,
- unacceptable risk,
- no accountable owner,
- inadequate data controls,
- no realistic review process,
- insufficient organizational readiness.

## AI Use Case Checklist

Before approving an AI marketing use case, confirm:

- [ ] A real business problem is defined
- [ ] The role of AI is clearly limited
- [ ] Required inputs are identified
- [ ] Input usage is approved
- [ ] Human review is documented
- [ ] Final accountability remains with a person
- [ ] Risks are assessed
- [ ] Mitigation actions are assigned
- [ ] Success criteria are measurable
- [ ] A process owner is confirmed
- [ ] Pilot scope is realistic
- [ ] Stop criteria are defined
- [ ] Organizational adoption is considered

## Key Principle

A valuable AI use case is not defined by the sophistication of the technology.

It is defined by its ability to improve a real business process through:

- relevant inputs,
- clear ownership,
- human judgment,
- controlled risk,
- measurable value,
- realistic organizational adoption.
